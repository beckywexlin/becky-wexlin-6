Buyer's guide

Comfort Colors vs Bella Canvas — which blank is actually better?

June 2026·8 min read

We get this question constantly: Comfort Colors or Bella Canvas? Which blank is better for graphic tees? We print on both at Becky Wexlin, so this isn't a theoretical comparison pulled from spec sheets. We've handled hundreds of each, worn them, washed them, printed on them, and listened to customers tell us which ones they reach for first. Here's the honest breakdown.

The short answer is that neither blank is objectively "better." They're built for different people who want different things from a t-shirt. The Comfort Colors 1717 and the Bella Canvas 3001 are both premium blanks, and both are miles ahead of the cheap, scratchy shirts you'd get from a bulk supplier. But they feel, fit, and print differently enough that the choice actually matters.

Let's get into the specifics.

The basics: specs side by side

Before we get into opinions, here are the raw numbers.

Comfort Colors 1717:

  • 6.1 oz fabric weight
  • 100% ringspun cotton, garment-dyed
  • Relaxed, boxy fit
  • Pre-shrunk
  • Twill-taped neck and shoulders

Bella Canvas 3001:

  • 4.2 oz fabric weight
  • 100% Airlume combed and ringspun cotton
  • Slim, retail-style fit
  • Side-seamed construction
  • Tear-away label

The weight difference alone tells you a lot. The Comfort Colors is nearly 50% heavier per square yard. You can feel it immediately when you pick one up. The Bella Canvas feels like a shirt you'd buy at a retail store. The Comfort Colors feels like a shirt you'd steal from your older sibling's closet in 1997.

Fabric feel

This is where the two blanks diverge most dramatically, and it's the thing people care about most.

Comfort Colors has a thick, broken-in feel right out of the bag. The garment-dyeing process gives the cotton a slightly textured, lived-in quality. It feels heavier on your body. If you've ever put on a vintage thrift store tee that felt substantial and soft at the same time, that's the vibe Comfort Colors is going for. The fabric has a slight nap to it, almost like a very fine fleece. It drapes loosely rather than clinging.

Bella Canvas takes the opposite approach. The Airlume combed cotton is engineered to be as smooth and soft as possible. It's lighter, thinner, and has that buttery hand feel that people associate with modern premium basics. Think of the blank tees you see at Everlane or Uniqlo — that silky, smooth, almost-too-soft feel. The fabric lays flat and moves with you rather than hanging off you.

Neither feel is wrong. But they're targeting very different preferences. If "soft" to you means thick and cozy, you want Comfort Colors. If "soft" means silky and smooth, you want Bella Canvas.

Fit

This is the second-biggest difference, and the one that catches people off guard if they're ordering online without trying both on first.

Comfort Colors runs large. The fit is relaxed and boxy through the chest, shoulders, and body. A medium in Comfort Colors will feel closer to what you'd expect from a large in most retail brands. The sleeves are wider, the body is longer, and the overall silhouette is loose. If you want a fitted look, size down. If you want that effortlessly oversized look that's been dominating for the past few years, go true to size or even size up.

Bella Canvas is true to size and leans slightly slim. A medium fits like a medium. The body is tapered through the torso, the sleeves are shorter and more fitted, and the overall shape is what you'd expect from a shirt at a modern retail store. If you're between sizes, size up for a relaxed fit. But generally, your normal size will work.

We hear from customers all the time who ordered a Comfort Colors in their usual size and were surprised by how roomy it was. It's not a defect — it's the design. That boxy, relaxed drape is the whole point.

Print quality

Both blanks are fully compatible with DTG (direct-to-garment) printing, which is what we use at Becky Wexlin for most of our designs. But the print results are noticeably different because the fabric surfaces are so different.

On Comfort Colors, prints have a slightly vintage, almost screen-printed look. The garment-dyed texture means the ink sits on a slightly uneven surface, which gives the design a soft, worn-in quality right from the first wear. Fine details and small text still come through, but the overall impression is warmer and more organic. If your design has a retro or hand-drawn aesthetic, it tends to look especially good on Comfort Colors.

On Bella Canvas, prints come out crisper and sharper. The smooth, flat fabric surface gives the ink a clean, even canvas to sit on. Colors appear more vibrant and edges are more defined. If your design has a lot of fine detail, photographic elements, or precise typography, Bella Canvas will render it more faithfully.

Neither result is better in absolute terms. It's more about what suits the design. We've had the same graphic look completely different on each blank — moodier and vintage on the CC, clean and punchy on the B+C. Both look great. They just look different.

Shrinkage

Nobody wants to spend $30+ on a graphic tee and have it shrink into a crop top after the first wash. Here's what we've seen with both.

Comfort Colors is pre-shrunk as part of the garment-dyeing process, so most of the shrinkage has already happened before the shirt reaches you. That said, if you consistently machine dry on high heat, you may notice about a half-size reduction over time, mostly in length. Washing cold and tumble drying low (or air drying) keeps them very close to their original dimensions.

Bella Canvas has minimal shrinkage out of the box. The Airlume cotton is pre-shrunk and holds its shape well through repeated washes. You might see very slight shrinkage on the first wash, but it's generally negligible. The side-seamed construction also helps the shirt keep its shape over time, since the seams act as structural reinforcement.

Both blanks hold up well if you follow basic care instructions: wash cold, tumble dry low, don't iron directly on the print. We have a full care guide if you want the details.

Which one should you pick?

Here's the simple framework.

Pick Comfort Colors if you want:

  • A heavyweight, substantial feel
  • That vintage, broken-in aesthetic
  • A relaxed, oversized fit
  • Prints that look a little retro and lived-in
  • A shirt that feels like you've owned it for years

Pick Bella Canvas if you want:

  • A lightweight, buttery-soft feel
  • A modern, slim retail fit
  • Clean, crisp, vibrant prints
  • A shirt that works tucked in or styled with tailored pieces
  • Minimal shrinkage and consistent sizing

There's genuinely no wrong answer here. We stock both because our customers want both. Some people are ride-or-die Comfort Colors fans who won't touch anything else. Others swear by Bella Canvas and love the way it fits. A few people have both in their closets and pick based on the outfit and the occasion, which is honestly the most reasonable approach.

Frequently asked questions

Is Comfort Colors better than Bella Canvas?

Neither is objectively better — they serve different preferences. Comfort Colors is ideal if you want a heavyweight, relaxed, vintage-feeling tee. Bella Canvas is the better pick if you prefer a softer, lighter, modern-fitted shirt. Both are premium blanks and both produce great graphic tees.

Do Comfort Colors shirts shrink?

Comfort Colors shirts are garment-dyed and pre-shrunk, so most of the shrinkage has already happened before the shirt reaches you. You may still see slight shrinkage (around half a size) if you machine dry on high heat consistently. Washing cold and tumble drying low will keep them close to their original size.

What weight is best for graphic tees?

It depends on the look and feel you want. Midweight blanks (4.0–4.5 oz) like the Bella Canvas 3001 give a soft, retail-quality feel with crisp prints. Heavyweight blanks (5.5–6.5 oz) like the Comfort Colors 1717 give a more substantial, vintage feel. For everyday graphic tees, anything in the 4.0–6.5 oz range on ringspun cotton is a solid choice.
