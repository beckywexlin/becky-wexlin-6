Care guide

How to care for printed graphic tees so they last

January 15, 2025·6 min read

You find the perfect graphic tee. You wear it twice. You throw it in the wash. Three months later the design is cracking, fading, and peeling at the edges — and you're back to square one. It doesn't have to be this way. Caring for a printed graphic tee takes almost no extra effort, and the difference it makes is enormous.

Why graphic tee prints degrade in the first place

Most graphic tees use one of two print methods: screen printing or direct-to-garment (DTG). Both produce great results when the shirt is new, but both are vulnerable to the same enemies: heat, friction, and harsh detergents.

Screen printing uses layers of ink applied through a mesh screen. The ink sits on top of the fabric fibres, which means aggressive washing or high heat can cause it to crack and flake over time. DTG printing works more like an inkjet printer — the ink is absorbed into the fabric fibres, so it's generally more durable, but it's still susceptible to fading if you're not careful about temperature.

Understanding this helps explain every rule that follows. You're not being precious about a shirt — you're working with the physics of how the print is constructed.

Wash inside out, every single time

This is the single highest-impact thing you can do, and it costs you nothing. Turning a graphic tee inside out before washing means the print faces away from the drum of the washing machine, other clothes, and the agitation that causes surface friction.

That friction — even at low temperatures — is one of the main causes of print degradation. Inside out washing eliminates most of it. Make it a reflex. You'll notice the difference within the first few months.

Cold water is your best friend

Hot water breaks down the bond between ink and fabric. This is true for both screen-printed and DTG tees. Washing your graphic tees in cold water (30°C / 86°F or below) dramatically extends the life of the print and also uses less energy — which is nice for the planet and your electricity bill.

If your washing machine has a "delicate" or "gentle" cycle, use it for your graphic tees. Less agitation means less friction means a longer-lasting print.

Skip the tumble dryer if you can

Heat is the enemy of printed fabric. Tumble dryers run at temperatures that are genuinely damaging to print quality over time, and the mechanical tumbling adds friction on top of the heat. If you can hang your graphic tees to air dry, do it.

If you have to use a dryer, use the lowest heat setting available and remove the shirts while they're still slightly damp. Hang them out to finish drying flat. This reduces total heat exposure significantly.

One more thing: don't hang graphic tees on wire hangers over a radiator. The concentrated heat at the shoulders is exactly where you don't want it, and the hanger can stretch the collar over time. Lay them flat or use a clothes horse away from direct heat.

Choose a gentle detergent

Biological detergents (the kind with enzymes) are great at removing stains but they're harder on fabric and print inks than non-biological alternatives. For your graphic tees, a gentle or non-bio detergent is the better choice.

Avoid fabric softener. It sounds counterintuitive — softer seems like it should mean more gentle — but fabric softener leaves a coating on fibres that can dull print colours over time and interfere with DTG prints in particular.

How to store graphic tees without damaging the print

Folding is better than hanging for most graphic tees. Hanging a cotton tee for extended periods can stretch the collar and shoulders, and if the shirt is slightly damp when hung (which happens more than you'd think), that moisture can cause the print to stick slightly to itself when folded later.

Fold your graphic tees print-side in so the design isn't rubbing against other items in the drawer. Store them somewhere dry — moisture and heat between wears is a slow form of the same damage that washing machines do.

What to do if the print starts to crack

Minor cracking on a screen-printed design doesn't mean the shirt is ruined. It means the top layers of ink are beginning to break down, which is normal wear over time. At this stage, switching to cold wash and air drying will slow further degradation significantly.

For DTG prints, fading rather than cracking is the more common issue. Again, cold wash and avoiding the dryer will slow this considerably. There's no way to reverse fading once it's happened, but you can stop it from progressing.

The shirts that last years are the ones that never saw the inside of a hot dryer.

None of this is complicated. Cold water, inside out, air dry when you can. That's genuinely most of it. The graphic tee you're wearing today can still look good in five years if you treat it right — and that's worth the thirty seconds it takes to flip it inside out before you throw it in the wash.
