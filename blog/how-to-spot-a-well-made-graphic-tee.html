Buyer's guide

How to spot a well-made graphic tee before you buy

March 1, 2025·8 min read

The graphic tee market is enormous and largely unregulated by quality. A $12 shirt and a $35 shirt can look identical in a product photo and feel completely different in your hands — and only one of them will still look good in a year. Here's a practical checklist for spotting the difference before you spend your money.

Start with what the brand tells you (and doesn't)

Before you evaluate a single physical detail, look at what the brand publishes about their shirts. Specifically:

  • GSM (grams per square metre) — this is the fabric weight. Anything below 160 GSM is on the lighter side for a graphic tee; 180 GSM and above is where quality starts to feel substantial. If a brand doesn't list GSM anywhere, that's often because the number isn't impressive.
  • Cotton type — ring-spun or combed ring-spun cotton is softer and more durable than standard cotton. If the product description just says "100% cotton" with no further detail, it's probably standard.
  • Print method — screen print or direct-to-garment (DTG) are both legitimate methods with different characteristics. Screen printing tends to produce more vibrant colours on simpler designs; DTG handles complex, photographic designs better. Either can be done well or badly.

A brand that's proud of their materials will tell you about them. Vague product descriptions are a yellow flag.

How to evaluate the print quality

When you can see the shirt in person, the print tells you almost everything you need to know about whether it was made with care.

Look at the edges of the design. Clean, crisp edges indicate proper ink viscosity and application. Blurry, bleeding, or fuzzy edges suggest the ink was too thin or the printing process wasn't well controlled.

Check for even coverage in solid areas. Areas of flat colour should be consistent — no patchiness, no thin spots where the fabric shows through. This is especially important to check in dark inks on light fabric, where unevenness is most visible.

Feel the print with your fingers. A good print should feel smooth and relatively flat against the fabric. If it feels thick, plasticky, or heavily raised, it's more likely to crack with washing. The best screen prints have a slight texture but don't feel like a separate object attached to the shirt.

Hold it up to the light. For lighter inks on darker fabric, this reveals whether the coverage is genuinely opaque or slightly transparent. Multiple passes of ink produce better opacity but cost more — which is why cheap shirts sometimes have ghost-like prints where the fabric colour bleeds through.

The fabric test: what to feel for

Quality fabric has a specific feel that becomes easy to recognise once you know what you're looking for. Here's what separates good from great:

  • Weight — a quality graphic tee should feel substantial in your hand. Not heavy like a sweatshirt, but not flimsy or papery either. If you can see your hand through the fabric when you hold it up, the GSM is too low.
  • Softness — ring-spun or combed cotton feels noticeably smoother than standard cotton. It shouldn't feel scratchy or rough against your wrist when you hold it there.
  • Recovery — stretch the fabric slightly between your hands and let go. Good quality cotton recovers its shape quickly and completely. Fabric that stays stretched or takes time to recover will do the same after wearing and washing.
  • Surface consistency — run your hand over the fabric. Good quality cotton feels smooth and even. Pilling, bobbling, or inconsistent texture before the shirt has even been worn is a sign of lower quality fibre.

Construction details that reveal build quality

The stitching on a t-shirt is where corners get cut most easily, and it's also where quality shows most clearly on close inspection.

Shoulder seams should be clean, reinforced, and sitting exactly at the edge of the shoulder. Pull gently on the seam — it should feel solid with no give. Cheap shoulder seams are single-stitched and will fail under repeated stress.

Side seams should run straight down the body without twisting toward the front or back. Twisted seams indicate a poorly constructed pattern or a factory cutting corners on alignment. It looks fine on a hanger and becomes obvious the moment you put it on.

The hem at the bottom of the shirt should lie flat and feel substantial. Double-stitched or coverstitched hems stay flat through washing. Single-stitched hems curl up over time and never recover.

The collar should feel structured without being stiff — ribbed construction holds shape better than plain knit. If the collar already feels stretched or misshapen on a new shirt, that's a reliable predictor of how it will look after twenty washes.

Fit indicators to check before you buy

A great shirt on a bad pattern is still a bad shirt. Here's what to look for in the cut:

  • The shoulder seam should sit at the edge of the shoulder bone — not drooping down the upper arm, not pulling up toward the neck.
  • The chest should have enough room to move naturally without pulling across the back when you bring your arms forward.
  • The length should be long enough that the shirt stays tucked-in-adjacent when you raise your arms — shirts that expose your midriff when you reach for something are cut too short.
  • The sleeves on a standard graphic tee should hit roughly mid-bicep — long enough to feel intentional, short enough to look clean.

Buying online: what to look for when you can't feel it

Most graphic tee purchases happen online, which removes your ability to do any of the physical tests above. Here's how to compensate:

Read the product description carefully for GSM and cotton type. If these aren't listed, contact the brand and ask — a company confident in their product will answer immediately. Look at product photos carefully for print edge quality and fabric drape. A shirt that drapes softly in product photos is generally heavier and better quality than one that hangs stiffly.

Read reviews specifically for mentions of quality after washing. "Great design, but faded after a few washes" tells you something the product photos never will. And if a brand has a clear, specific returns policy for quality issues, that's a good sign they're confident enough in the product to stand behind it.

The graphic tee you wear for years is the one you bought once, carefully, from someone who knew what they were making.

Once you know what you're looking for, it becomes much easier to make buying decisions quickly and confidently. The shirts worth owning are out there — you just need to know what to check.
