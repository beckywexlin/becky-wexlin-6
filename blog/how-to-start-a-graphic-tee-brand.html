Behind the scenes

How to start a graphic tee brand in 2026 (from someone who actually did it)

June 2026·10 min read

There are roughly a thousand articles on the internet titled "How to Start a T-Shirt Business." Most of them are written by people who sell courses about starting t-shirt businesses, not by people who actually ship t-shirts. This is the other kind. The kind written by someone who has a garage with boxes in it, a Printify account with real order history, and a brand that started in 2024 in Santa Barbara, California.

Becky Wexlin is a weird graphic tee brand. We make shirts with absurd designs, aggressive puns, and illustrations that don't quite make sense until you've looked at them for a second too long. We're not a case study in explosive growth. We're a case study in actually doing this thing, day after day, and learning what works by getting a lot of it wrong first.

If you're thinking about starting a graphic tee brand, here's what the process actually looks like. No hacks, no funnels, no "passive income" promises. Just the real steps, the real costs, and the stuff nobody mentions in the course.

Step 1 — Figure out what makes you different

This is the part everyone skips, and it's the only part that actually matters.

Here's the reality: everyone has access to the same blank t-shirts. Everyone can use the same print-on-demand services. Everyone can set up a Shopify store in an afternoon. The barriers to entry are essentially zero, which means the barriers to being interesting are the only ones that count.

Your designs and your brand voice are your moat. That's it. Not your supplier, not your platform, not your pricing strategy. If someone could swap your logo onto a competitor's shirts and nobody would notice the difference, you don't have a brand yet. You have a store.

When we started Becky Wexlin, the differentiator was what we call "architecture of the absurd." Our shirts aren't random funny slogans slapped on cotton. They're built around a specific sensibility — deadpan humor, visual jokes that reward a second look, the kind of shirt that makes a stranger at the coffee shop say "wait, what does your shirt say?" and then laugh once they get it. Every design has to pass that test. If it doesn't make someone stop, it doesn't ship.

You don't need our specific angle. But you need an angle. Maybe it's hyper-niche (shirts for birdwatchers, shirts for people who homebrew, shirts for civil engineers who are tired). Maybe it's an art style nobody else is doing. Maybe it's a tone of voice. Whatever it is, figure it out before you order your first sample, because everything else flows downstream from this decision.

Step 2 — Choose your blanks wisely

The blank — the actual shirt your design gets printed on — matters more than most new brands realize. A great design on a cheap, scratchy, boxy blank is a shirt that gets worn once and then lives in the back of a drawer. A good design on a shirt that actually feels nice is a shirt someone reaches for on a Saturday morning.

Here are the blanks we've worked with and what we've learned about each:

Comfort Colors 1717. The darling of the garment-dyed world. Heavyweight (6.1 oz), ringspun cotton, preshrunk, and available in an absurd range of colors. The hand feel is soft out of the bag and gets softer with every wash. The vintage, slightly faded aesthetic is built into the fabric. This is the blank that made "comfort colors" a search term. Downside: it's not cheap, and the fit runs slightly boxy.

Bella Canvas 3001. The most popular retail-fit blank in the DTG world. Lighter weight (4.2 oz), side-seamed, and fitted without being tight. It drapes well and looks clean. This is what you pick if your brand leans more modern and less vintage. The fabric is a cotton-poly blend on some colors, which affects print quality, so check the specs.

Gildan 5000. The workhorse. Affordable, available everywhere, decent quality for the price. If you're testing designs and don't want to spend $10+ per blank, the Gildan 5000 is where a lot of brands start. It's not going to win any awards for softness, but it gets the job done. Heavy cotton (5.3 oz), classic fit.

AS Colour. Australian brand, increasingly popular in the U.S. The Staple Tee is mid-weight, side-seamed, and has a more tailored fit than most American blanks. Great quality. Slightly more limited color range and higher cost, but the fit and fabric are genuinely premium.

Cotton Heritage. A solid mid-range option. Their premium blanks compete with Comfort Colors on softness at a slightly lower price point. Worth sampling if you're cost-conscious but don't want to sacrifice quality.

What actually matters when choosing: fabric weight (measured in ounces — 4 oz is lightweight, 6+ oz is heavyweight), cotton type (ringspun is softer than open-end), and fit profile (classic/boxy vs. retail/fitted). You can absolutely start with one blank and expand later. We'd recommend picking the one that fits your brand's vibe, ordering samples, wearing them, washing them, and then deciding. Step 3 — Print-on-demand vs. inventory

This is the fork in the road that every new tee brand hits, and the answer depends entirely on your risk tolerance and your cash flow.

Print-on-demand (POD) means a service like Printify or Printful holds the blanks, prints your design when an order comes in, and ships it directly to the customer. You never touch the shirt. The advantages are real: zero inventory risk, no upfront cost per design, global fulfillment networks, and the ability to offer dozens of designs without storing anything. The downside is also real: your margins are lower, because you're paying the POD provider's markup on both the blank and the printing. And you have less control over quality, packaging, and turnaround time.

Inventory means you buy blanks in bulk, get them printed (screen printing for volume, DTG for small runs), and store and ship them yourself or through a 3PL. The margins are significantly better — a screen-printed Comfort Colors tee at volume might cost you $6-8 all in, versus $15+ through POD. But you're committing capital upfront, guessing at size ratios, and storing boxes of shirts somewhere.

Becky Wexlin uses POD through Printify. We made that choice early and we've stuck with it. The margins are tighter, but we can test new designs with zero financial risk, and we don't have to predict which size of which design in which color is going to sell. For a brand that launches new designs regularly and doesn't want to sit on dead inventory, POD is the right call. If you're running a smaller catalog with proven bestsellers, the inventory model starts to make more sense.

Be honest with yourself about where you are. If this is your first brand and you don't have $5,000 to put into inventory, start with POD. You can always move to inventory later once you know what sells.

Step 4 — Build your store

You have three realistic options: Shopify, Etsy, or your own site.

Shopify is the default recommendation and it's a good one. It handles payments, inventory (or POD integration), shipping, and gives you a professional storefront out of the box. The basic plan is around $39/month plus transaction fees. If you want a functioning store with minimal technical effort, this is the move.

Etsy has a massive built-in audience searching for exactly the kind of thing you're selling. The listing fees are small, the transaction fees are not (6.5% plus payment processing). But the traffic is real. A lot of graphic tee brands get their first sales on Etsy, and there's no shame in that. The tradeoff: you're building on someone else's platform. Your customers are Etsy customers, not your customers. You don't own the relationship.

Your own site is the hardest path and arguably the most valuable long-term. Becky Wexlin runs on a custom-built site hosted on Cloudflare Pages. No monthly platform fees, no transaction fees beyond payment processing, and complete control over the brand experience. The downside is that you have to build the thing, and you have to drive your own traffic. Nobody's going to find you by browsing.

Our recommendation: start on Etsy to validate demand and get early sales. Build your own site (even on Shopify) as soon as you can, and start directing traffic there. Owning your platform matters for brand building, email capture, SEO, and margins. Etsy is a channel; your site is your home.

Step 5 — Price it right

This is where most new brands get it wrong, and it's usually by pricing too low.

Let's do the math that nobody wants to do. If you're using POD, a typical cost structure looks something like this:

Blank t-shirt: ~$8. Printing (DTG): ~$5. Shipping to customer: ~$4. Payment processing (3%): ~$0.75 on a $25 shirt. Platform fees (if applicable): varies.

That puts your hard cost at roughly $17-18 per shirt before you've paid yourself, paid for your design work, paid for your website, paid for any marketing, or bought lunch. A $25 shirt at those margins gives you about $7-8 of profit. Sell ten shirts in a week and you've made $70. That's not nothing, but it's not a salary either.

You have two levers: volume and price. Most successful indie tee brands price between $28 and $38, depending on the blank and the perceived brand value. Premium blanks like Comfort Colors can justify higher prices. A brand with strong design and clear identity can charge more than a generic "funny shirt" shop. Don't race to the bottom on price — you'll just get there faster.

Be transparent with your customers about quality. People will pay $34 for a shirt if they understand they're getting a garment-dyed, heavyweight, ringspun cotton tee with an original design. They won't pay $34 for something they think is a $15 Gildan with clip art on it. Your product page has to do the work of explaining the value.

Step 6 — Get people to find you

The hardest part of running a graphic tee brand isn't making the shirts. It's getting anyone to see them.

SEO is a slow burn but it compounds. Write blog content that answers real questions people are searching for. (You're reading an example of that right now.) Product pages with detailed descriptions, proper titles, and real photography rank better than pages with a single mockup and three words of copy. It takes months to build organic traffic, but once it's there, it doesn't cost you per click.

Social media is where most tee brands start, and it works — but not the way you think. Posting a product photo and saying "link in bio" is not a strategy. Showing the design process, the weird ideas that didn't make it, the behind-the-scenes of running a tiny brand from your apartment — that's content people actually follow. The algorithm rewards consistency and authenticity, which is convenient because those are free.

Email is the channel you own. Build your list from day one. Every visitor who gives you their email is someone you can talk to directly, without paying for ads or hoping the algorithm shows your post. Send new design launches, tell the story behind the designs, be a human being in their inbox. Email converts better than any other channel for small e-commerce brands, and it's not close.

And here's the thing nobody puts in the marketing section: your designs are your marketing. If someone wears your shirt and someone else asks "where'd you get that?" — that's the engine. That's the entire flywheel. Make shirts that are interesting enough to start a conversation, and the conversation does the marketing for you.

What nobody tells you — it's slow

The first six months are quiet. Really quiet. You'll sell to friends and family. You'll check your analytics dashboard twelve times a day and see the same single-digit numbers. You'll wonder if anyone besides your mom has ever visited your store. This is normal.

Every successful indie brand you admire went through this phase. They just don't talk about it because it's not interesting content. The reality is that building a brand — a real brand, not a dropshipping experiment — is a slow, compounding process. Your tenth design is better than your first. Your hundredth product photo is better than your tenth. Your website gets a little more organic traffic every month. One customer tells a friend. That friend tells another friend. It builds.

Don't quit your day job yet. Seriously. Run this on the side until the revenue justifies the leap. The brands that survive aren't the ones that went viral on TikTok once — they're the ones that kept showing up, kept shipping, kept making shirts that were a little better and a little weirder than the last batch.

Becky Wexlin started as a side project. It still feels like a side project some days, just one that happens to ship to all fifty states. The brand builds one shirt at a time. That's not a limitation. That's the whole point.
