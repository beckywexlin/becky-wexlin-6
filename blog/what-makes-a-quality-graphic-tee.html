Buyer's guide

What actually makes a quality graphic tee?

January 22, 2025·7 min read

Most people can feel the difference between a good graphic tee and a bad one the moment they pick it up. But knowing what you're actually feeling — and why it matters — helps you make smarter decisions and stops you from wasting money on shirts that look great on the hanger and disintegrate by summer's end.

Fabric weight: the number that matters most

Fabric weight is measured in GSM — grams per square metre. It's the single most useful number when evaluating a t-shirt, and most people have never heard of it.

Here's a rough guide to what the numbers mean:

  • Under 140 GSM — very lightweight, almost see-through. Fine for warm weather, but not durable and generally not great for print quality.
  • 140–160 GSM — the budget range. Most fast fashion graphic tees live here. Wears out faster, prints don't hold as well.
  • 160–200 GSM — the sweet spot for graphic tees. Substantial enough to hold a print well, comfortable across most temperatures, durable with proper care.
  • 200 GSM and above — premium weight. Feels closer to a sweatshirt fabric, very durable, holds colour and print exceptionally well.

When a brand doesn't list the GSM of their shirts anywhere, that's usually a sign the number isn't one they're proud of.

Cotton type: combed vs. ring-spun vs. standard

Not all cotton is the same. The way it's processed changes how the final fabric feels, how it wears, and how well it holds a print.

Standard cotton is the baseline. It works, it's cheap to produce, and it's what most budget graphic tees are made from. The fibres are shorter and less refined, which means a slightly rougher texture and less durability over time.

Ring-spun cotton is processed to align the fibres more tightly before spinning into yarn. The result is a smoother, stronger fabric that feels noticeably softer and holds its shape better through repeated washing. Most quality graphic tees use ring-spun cotton.

Combed cotton goes a step further — short fibres are combed out before spinning, leaving only the longest, smoothest fibres. The result is an exceptionally soft, durable fabric. Combed and ring-spun together is the gold standard for printed apparel.

Fabric blend: 100% cotton vs. cotton-polyester

100% cotton breathes well, feels natural against the skin, and is the preferred choice for most graphic tee enthusiasts. The trade-off is that it wrinkles easily and can shrink if washed hot.

Cotton-polyester blends (typically 50/50 or 60/40 cotton-poly) are more resistant to shrinking and wrinkling, and they tend to hold their shape slightly better. Some people find the feel less natural. For graphic printing, blends can produce slightly different results depending on the print method used.

Tri-blend fabrics (cotton, polyester, and rayon) produce a very soft, slightly vintage-feeling fabric that many premium brands favour. They tend to drape well and feel broken-in from the first wear.

Print quality: what to look for

The quality of the print is as important as the quality of the fabric — you're buying both. Here's what separates good printing from great printing:

  • Crisp edges — clean lines without bleeding or blurring at the edges of the design indicate proper ink viscosity and application technique.
  • Even coverage — no patchiness or thin spots in areas of solid colour. This matters especially in dark ink on light fabric.
  • Hand feel — a high-quality print should feel smooth, not plasticky or raised. Thick, rubbery prints tend to crack faster.
  • Colour depth — vibrant colours that look the same in person as in the product photo indicate good colour management during printing.

Construction: the details most people miss

The stitching on a t-shirt tells you a lot about how it was made and how long it will last.

Look at the shoulder seams. On a well-constructed tee, these should be reinforced — double-stitched or taped — because the shoulder is where most stress occurs during wear. A single-stitched shoulder seam on a cheap shirt will pull apart over time.

Check the hem. A double-stitched or coverstitched hem lies flat, resists curling, and holds its shape through repeated washing. A single-stitched hem will roll after a few washes and never recover.

The collar should feel structured without being stiff. Ribbed collars hold their shape better than non-ribbed ones, and a collar that's been reinforced with tape or a second layer of fabric will resist stretching over time.

Fit and cut: often overlooked

A great fabric and a great print are wasted on a bad cut. The fit of a graphic tee is subjective — some people prefer a boxy fit, others a more tailored silhouette — but there are objective markers of a well-cut shirt.

The shoulder seam should sit at the edge of your shoulder, not drooping down your arm. The body shouldn't be so short that it untucks when you raise your arms. Side seams that run straight rather than twisting around to the front indicate a well-constructed pattern.

Unisex cuts are the standard for most graphic tees, and they work well when the cut is generous through the body without being shapeless. A good unisex tee drapes naturally and looks intentional, not like you borrowed it from someone else.

The best graphic tee is the one you still want to wear in three years.

Quality in a graphic tee isn't complicated — it's fabric weight, cotton processing, print method, and construction. Once you know what to look for, you'll spot the difference immediately. And once you've worn a genuinely well-made graphic tee, it's hard to go back to anything less.
