Buyer's guide

Why are graphic tees so expensive now?

May 9, 2026·10 min read

The short answer

Because everything that goes into making one costs more than it used to, and the shirts that actually feel good were never cheap to begin with.

A graphic tee from a small brand at $35–$45 isn't a rip-off. It's the honest math of cotton, shipping, print, and the fact that nobody making it has Walmart's leverage. The bad news is your sense of what a shirt "should" cost was set by an industry that mostly stopped existing.

Here's what's actually going on.

Cotton costs more than it used to

Cotton is a global commodity, and its price has been quietly climbing — slowly, then all at once. Drought, supply-chain disruption, and rising production costs in major cotton-growing regions have pushed raw material prices up significantly over the last several years. A heavyweight, ring-spun, garment-dyed cotton blank costs the brand selling it more than it cost five years ago. That cost gets passed on. Not because anyone wants to charge you more, but because the math no longer works otherwise.

Shipping and fulfillment went up and never came back down

The post-pandemic shipping landscape is the gift that keeps not giving. Container rates, fuel surcharges, last-mile delivery — almost every link in the chain between a shirt getting printed and arriving on your doorstep costs more than it did in 2019. Free shipping isn't free. Someone is paying for it, and that someone is usually the brand, who builds it into the price.

If you've noticed shirts crept up by five or eight bucks between 2021 and now, this is most of the reason.

Modern print methods cost more per shirt

The cracked, peeling screen print on your old college tee was cheap to produce because screen printing at scale is cheap — but it requires bulk runs, screens, ink mixing, and setup. For a small brand printing on demand or in short runs, screen printing usually doesn't pencil out.

The modern alternatives — DTG (direct-to-garment) and DTF (direct-to-film) — let small brands print one shirt at a time without sacrificing quality. The per-shirt cost is just higher than a 500-shirt screen run. You're paying for the flexibility. That's the trade. We covered the differences in detail on our shirt guide.

Small brands don't have Walmart's economics

This is the one nobody likes to say out loud.

The reason a graphic tee at a giant retailer can cost eight dollars is that giant retailers buy hundreds of thousands of shirts at once, often produced under conditions you'd rather not picture, with minimal labour cost, in fabric that prioritises price over feel. Their margins are thin and their volume is enormous.

A small apparel brand has none of that. Higher per-unit cost on the blank, more expensive print method, lower volume, no shared logistics, and one or two actual humans answering emails. The price reflects the operation behind it.

This isn't a moral argument about which is better. It's just arithmetic. If a graphic tee costs $40, you're not being scammed — you're paying the actual cost of a shirt made by people you could probably reach if you tried.

Better blanks cost more, and you can feel the difference

The blank shirt under the graphic is doing more work than people realise.

A 6-ounce garment-dyed Comfort Colors tee costs the brand significantly more than a 4-ounce import-grade basic. It also feels significantly better, lasts significantly longer, and holds a print significantly better. When you compare a $12 graphic tee to a $40 graphic tee, the design might be similar but the shirt underneath is on a different planet.

The short version: heavier, ring-spun, garment-dyed cotton is what a "premium" graphic tee actually is. And it's not cheap to source.

What you're actually paying for

When you buy a $40 graphic tee from a small brand, the breakdown looks roughly like this. Every brand is a little different, but the shape holds:

  • A meaningful chunk goes to the blank shirt itself
  • Another meaningful chunk goes to the print
  • Shipping, packaging, and fulfilment take their slice
  • Payment processors quietly skim about 3%
  • Platform and listing fees take more
  • Whatever's left covers the design work and the brand's actual margin

The brand isn't keeping $35 of your $40. They're rarely keeping $10. The sticker shock makes more sense once you see the receipt.

When an expensive graphic tee is worth it

Cost-per-wear is the only metric that matters for clothes.

A $45 shirt that lasts five years and gets worn weekly costs you about 17 cents per wear. A $12 shirt that loses its print after eight washes and shrinks into an unrecognisable shape costs you a lot more than 17 cents per wear, and ends up in a landfill faster.

The expensive graphic tee is worth it when:

  • The fabric weight is actually substantial (5+ ounces, ideally 6+)
  • The print method is solid and the design is well-registered
  • You'll wear it often enough for the cost-per-wear to land somewhere reasonable
  • You'd be genuinely annoyed if it vanished

When it isn't

Sometimes it isn't, and we're not going to pretend otherwise.

An expensive graphic tee isn't worth it when:

  • The fabric feels suspiciously thin for the price
  • The print is on a cheap blank dressed up with a fancy hangtag
  • The brand is charging premium prices for outsourced design and import-grade shirts
  • You're buying it for a one-time event and won't wear it again

Plenty of "premium" brands are charging $50 for a shirt that costs them seven dollars to make, and the only thing premium about it is the marketing. Trust your hands. If the shirt feels light and slick when you pick it up, the price probably isn't honest.

The bottom line

Graphic tees got more expensive because cotton got more expensive, shipping got more expensive, on-demand print methods cost more per unit than bulk runs, and small brands don't have Walmart's leverage.

A good graphic tee from a small brand is rarely a rip-off. A bad graphic tee from a big brand at the same price often is. The trick is learning to tell the difference, which mostly comes down to picking up the shirt and being honest about how it feels.

The shirts worth paying for are the ones you'll still be reaching for in five years. The shirts that aren't are the ones falling apart by next summer.
